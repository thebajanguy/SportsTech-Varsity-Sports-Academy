16-Core Course Mapping

  • English (4) — literature, composition, language.
  • Math (3) — Algebra I or higher (e.g., Algebra II, Geometry).
  • Science (2) — natural/physical sciences; include 1 lab if offered.
  • Additional English/Math/Science (1) — reinforces core academics.
  • Social Science (2) — history, government, geography, economics.
  • Additional Core (4) — foreign language, philosophy, or extra from above.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many talented athletes lose out on opportunities due to simple, avoidable mistakes. These errors can cost valuable scholarship chances, delay recruitment, or even block NCAA eligibility altogether.

  • Insufficient core courses or wrong subject mix
  • Waiting until senior year to check requirements
  • Missing official submissions (transcripts/test scores)
  • Competitions that risk amateur status
  • Assuming international credits always count

NCAA Compliance FAQ

Answers for student-athletes in Barbados and the Caribbean navigating NCAA eligibility.

Do CAPE subjects count toward NCAA eligibility?

Yes—CAPE Unit 1 and Unit 2 courses can count toward NCAA core requirements when they appear as approved core courses on your high school’s NCAA list. Final recognition depends on the course title, level, and content that your school submitted to the NCAA. We’ll map your CAPE subjects to the appropriate English, Math, Natural/Physical Science, Social Science, and additional academic categories.

Do CSEC subjects count?

CSEC General Proficiency subjects typically count when they are academic (not vocational) and listed as approved core courses for your school. Grades I–III are commonly recognized. We’ll verify each CSEC course against your school’s NCAA-approved list and place it in the right core area.

How many NCAA core courses do I need?

Division I and II require 16 core courses. For Division I, plan to complete 10 of the 16 before your 7th semester (start of your final school year), and at least 7 of those 10 in English, Math, or Natural/Physical Science. We’ll build your term-by-term plan.

What GPA is required for NCAA eligibility?

NCAA uses your core-course GPA (not overall GPA). As a rule of thumb: Division I targets a minimum core GPA around 2.3, and Division II around 2.2. Exact qualification depends on the NCAA sliding scale and your completion of the required core subjects. We’ll calculate your official core-GPA and set term targets.

Do I still need SAT/ACT scores?

For NCAA Division I, standardized test scores are not required for initial eligibility. However, individual colleges, admissions, or scholarships may still require or prefer scores. We’ll advise based on your target schools and provide prep support if testing strengthens your profile.

How do Caribbean transcripts get evaluated?

We gather your official CSEC/CAPE results and school transcripts, ensure course titles match the NCAA-approved list, and submit certified documents to the NCAA Eligibility Center. If needed, we include grading scales and school profiles so evaluators can interpret marks correctly.

What’s the NCAA Eligibility Center and when should I register?

It’s the NCAA’s portal for academic and amateurism certification. Create a profile by Form 4/Grade 10, then switch to a Certification Account when you’re being actively recruited or planning official visits. We’ll walk you through fees, documents, and timelines.

Can I accept prize money or play with older athletes and stay eligible?

Amateurism rules allow certain reimbursements but prohibit pay for play and professional contracts. Details vary by sport and circumstance. We’ll review your competitions, team status, and any payments to keep you compliant.

What if my school’s course isn’t on the NCAA list?

Only courses on the NCAA-approved core list for your school count. We’ll coordinate with your guidance office to update titles/descriptions or find approved alternatives (including CAPE units) so you stay on track.

How long does evaluation take and when should I start?

Start in Form 3–4 (Grades 9–10) to plan core subjects early. Document reviews can take weeks, especially with international exams. We build a timeline for registrations, exam sittings, and document submissions.
